## 2. The Problem (Ordinary World)

### Current Situation
Alex leads a high-performing data science team at TechFlow Analytics. They've been together 3.5 years, survived a previous liftout from DataCorp Inc, and have a cohesion score of 94/100. They've delivered $2.1M in annual savings through predictive modeling.

But TechFlow is stagnating. The company raised a modest Series A two years ago and hasn't grown since. The executive team is risk-averse. Alex sees diminishing career trajectory and limited scope for his team.

### What's Not Working
- **Career ceiling**: Alex has been "VP of Data Science" in title for a year, but still reports to a non-technical COO who doesn't understand their work
- **Limited scope**: Team is stuck doing fraud detection optimization; no pathway to strategic AI initiatives
- **Compensation compression**: Market rates have jumped 25% in 3 years; TechFlow won't match
- **Attrition risk**: Sarah got an offer from Anthropic last month. She stayed for the team, but how long?
- **Innovation blocked**: Every proposal for new ML projects dies in committee

### Failed Alternatives
1. **Individual job search**: Alex got a VP offer at a Series B fintech. $350K + equity. But it meant leaving Sarah, Marcus, and Priya behind. He turned it down.
2. **Internal advocacy**: Pitched a "Strategic AI Lab" to leadership. Got a "maybe next quarter" that's been repeated for 18 months.
3. **Side projects**: Team built an internal tool on weekends that could be productized. Leadership won't spin it out.
4. **Waiting it out**: Hoped TechFlow would get acquired. But no movement in 2 years.

### Emotional State
- **Frustrated**: "I've given this company 5 years. They don't see what we could become."
- **Guilty**: "If I leave, I'm breaking up the best team I've ever built."
- **Anxious**: "Sarah's getting calls from Google every week. I can't lose her."
- **Stuck**: "Individual success feels like betrayal. Staying feels like decay."

### The Core Tension
> "Every career framework tells me to optimize for myself. But my best work happens with this team. Why does advancing mean abandoning them?"

## 3. Discovery (Call to Adventure)

### The Trigger
Sarah forwards Alex an article from The American Lawyer about Crowell & Moring hiring 40+ lawyers as a team from Reed Smith. The headline: "Group laterals up 14% as teams refuse to break up."

Alex's reaction: "Wait, this is a thing? Whole teams can move together?"

### How Alex Found Liftout
1. **Week 1**: Googles "team-based hiring" and "group lateral moves tech"
2. **Week 2**: Finds Harvard Business Review article "Lift Outs: How to Acquire a High-Functioning Team" by Boris Groysberg
3. **Week 2**: Discovers Groysberg's "Chasing Stars" research: stars decline 46% when they move alone
4. **Week 3**: Searches "team hiring platform" and finds Liftout
5. **Week 3**: Reads "What is a Liftout?" page, sees 2024-2025 examples

### Initial Skepticism
- "Is this even legal? Won't we get sued?"
- "Companies won't pay 4x to hire a team when they could hire us individually"
- "Our employer will find out we're looking"
- "This sounds too good to be true"

### The Aha Moment
Reading the landing page: **"Your employer never sees you."**

Then: **"Teams that move together negotiate better compensation packages, maintain their track record, and skip the 12-month team-building phase."**

Alex realizes: This isn't about him getting a new job. This is about his team getting a new home.

## 6. Transformation (The Ordeal)

### Key Decision Points

**Decision 1: Tell the team**
Alex had to convince Sarah, Marcus, and Priya to join him on Liftout. This required vulnerability: admitting he was looking to leave, and asking them to take that risk with him.

**Decision 2: Go from anonymous to selective**
After 3 weeks anonymous, Alex switched to "selective visibility" - allowing specific companies to see their profile while blocking competitors.

**Decision 3: Respond to NextGen Financial**
First real inquiry came from Sarah Rodriguez at NextGen. Alex had to decide: was this team ready to actually talk to companies?

**Decision 4: Negotiate as a unit**
NextGen wanted to interview team members separately. Alex pushed back: "We interview together or not at all."

### Moments of Doubt
- **Week 2**: Marcus asked, "What if we get blackballed in the industry for doing this?"
- **Week 4**: Radio silence after first inquiry. Alex wondered if they'd been too aggressive on salary.
- **Week 6**: TechFlow announced a re-org. Alex panicked that their manager knew.
- **Week 8**: Counter-offer from another company wanted only Alex, not the team. Tempting number.

### What Kept Them Going
1. **Mutual accountability**: Once all 4 were on the platform, no one wanted to be the one who quit
2. **Market validation**: AI matching showed 88%+ fit with 3 opportunities
3. **Sarah's conviction**: "I've turned down 5 offers to stay with this team. This is the only way I'd actually move."
4. **Sunk cost**: They'd already done the work of building the profile

### Breakthroughs
- **First interview**: NextGen Financial's VP was clearly excited about getting a "ready-made" team
- **Compensation discussion**: Package for 4 was higher per-person than individual offers they'd received
- **The "we're in" moment**: All 4 team members agreed to accept the offer together
