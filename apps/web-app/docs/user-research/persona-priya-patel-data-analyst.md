## 2. The Problem (Ordinary World)

### Current Situation
Priya was 22 when she joined Alex's team—fresh out of UC Davis, eager to prove herself. Now 26, she's the glue between technical work and business value. She translates Sarah's models into executive dashboards. She turns Marcus's infrastructure metrics into ROI stories.

She's also the youngest person in every room, and she feels it.

### Why Priya Stays (For Now)
- **Growth**: "I've learned more in 3.5 years here than I would have in 10 at a big consulting firm."
- **Respect**: "Alex treats me like a peer, not an intern."
- **Mentorship**: "Sarah teaches me ML. Marcus teaches me systems thinking. It's free grad school."
- **Ownership**: "I own stakeholder reporting. That's rare for someone my age."

### What's Not Working
- **Career ceiling**: There's no "Lead Analyst" role at TechFlow. Nowhere to go.
- **Title gap**: She does senior work but sometimes feels perceived as junior.
- **Compensation lag**: Her friends in consulting make $180-200K. She's at $160K.
- **Management track**: She wants to lead a team. TechFlow has no analyst team to lead.
- **Imposter syndrome**: "Am I good, or am I just lucky to be on a good team?"

### The Generational Challenge
Priya is the youngest by 3+ years. She worries:
- "Do they invite me to the liftout because they value me, or because it's awkward to leave me behind?"
- "Am I ready for this? What if I can't perform at a new company?"
- "What if I'm the weak link and the deal falls through because of me?"

### Emotional State
- **Excited but nervous**: "This is an adventure, but I'm scared."
- **Grateful but ambitious**: "I appreciate this team, and I want more."
- **Confident but doubting**: "I know I add value, but do *they* know?"
- **Loyal but restless**: "I'll stay if they're staying. But I want to grow."

### The Core Tension
> "I'm ready for my first management role. But I don't want to become a manager at a company full of strangers. I want to grow with people who've already invested in me."

## 3. Discovery (Call to Adventure)

### The Trigger
Priya gets Sarah's text: "Alex showed me something. I think we can all move together. Are you in?"

Her immediate reaction: "Wait, they want *me* too?"

### Priya's Initial Surprise
She assumed liftouts were for senior people—the PhDs, the leads, the people with leverage. Not the junior analyst who joined out of college.

When Alex explained, "We're a package deal. All four or none," Priya felt something new: indispensable.

### What Made It Click
Priya realized the team's value proposition depended on balance:
- **Alex**: Leadership and strategy
- **Sarah**: Research and credibility
- **Marcus**: Infrastructure and production
- **Priya**: Business translation and stakeholder trust

Without her, they're a technical team that can't communicate with executives. With her, they're a full-stack analytics division.

### The First Meeting
Alex sat down with each team member individually. Priya's conversation:

**Alex**: "We want to explore this, but only if you're comfortable. You're the newest member—this is a big decision."

**Priya**: "I'm in, but I have a question. Why do you want *me*? You could find another analyst."

**Alex**: "Because you're not 'another analyst.' You've been with us 3.5 years. You know how we work. You've built relationships with our stakeholders. We can't replicate that."

Priya cried. She'd never felt that valued.

## 6. Transformation (The Ordeal)

### Priya's Key Decisions

**Decision 1: Ask for Management Track**
Priya told Alex her condition: "I want to manage people within a year. If that's not possible, I'm less excited about moving."

Alex made it a team requirement: "Our analyst needs a growth path. We want confirmation there's a team for her to build."

**Decision 2: Negotiate Assertively**
When NextGen's first offer came in at $180K, Priya pushed back—her first real negotiation.

"I understand the offer is $180K. My research shows that Lead Analysts at Series B fintechs in the Bay Area average $195-210K. I'm asking for $200K."

NextGen countered at $200K. Priya got a 25% raise by asking.

**Decision 3: Own Her Value in Interviews**
Priya stopped saying "I'm just the analyst." She started saying:

"I translate our team's technical work into executive decisions. I'm the reason the CEO knows what our models do. I've documented $2.1M in savings because I understand what leadership needs to hear."

**Decision 4: Embrace Being the Youngest**
When the NextGen CTO asked about her experience, Priya reframed:

"I have 3.5 years of experience, all with this team. I've never worked anywhere else. That means I've spent my entire career learning from the best people I know. I'm not starting over—I'm growing with them."

### The Breakthrough
The joint interview. Priya presented the team's business impact—$2.1M savings, executive dashboards, stakeholder feedback.

The CFO leaned forward: "You built those dashboards?"

Priya: "I built them. Sarah and Marcus built what they visualize."

CFO: "We need someone like you. Our data scientists are brilliant but can't present to the board."

Priya realized: She wasn't the support act. She was the bridge they desperately needed.

Priya doesn't just make dashboards. She makes technical work legible to executives.

**Example**: Sarah builds a churn prediction model with 87% accuracy.

| Technical description | Priya's translation |

Priya inverts this. She tells executives what they need to know before they ask.
