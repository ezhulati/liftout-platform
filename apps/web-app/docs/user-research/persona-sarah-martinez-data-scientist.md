## 2. The Problem (Ordinary World)

### Current Situation
Sarah is the most technically accomplished member of Alex's team. Her Stanford PhD and NLP expertise make her highly sought after—Google, Anthropic, and OpenAI have all reached out in the past year. She could command $350K+ at any of them.

But Sarah hasn't left. Not because she can't. Because she won't.

### Why Sarah Stays (For Now)
- **Alex is the best manager she's had**: "He actually understands what I do. Most managers just want dashboards."
- **The team dynamic works**: "We disagree productively. That's rare."
- **Her work matters here**: "At TechFlow, I own the NLP roadmap. At Google, I'd be a cog."
- **Psychological safety**: "I can say 'I don't know' without being judged."

### What's Not Working
- **Compensation gap**: Market rate for her skills is $320-350K. She's at $240K.
- **No research time**: TechFlow wants production features, not papers. She misses research.
- **Career ceiling**: There's no Principal Data Scientist role at TechFlow. No staff track.
- **Aging parents**: Her parents are in San Jose. She can't relocate—this limits options.
- **Undervalued expertise**: Leadership doesn't understand NLP. They keep asking for "simple dashboards."

### The Recruiter Problem
Sarah gets 3-5 recruiter messages per week. Her responses:

| Offer Type | Her Response |
|------------|--------------|
| "Amazing opportunity at [Big Tech]" | "Can my team come too?" → No → Declined |
| "Lead a team at [Startup]" | "I don't want to leave my current team" → Ghosted |
| "Remote, great comp, equity" | "Interesting, but I'd be starting over socially" → Declined |
| "We're building AGI" | "So is everyone. Pass." |

### Emotional State
- **Loyal but frustrated**: "I love this team. I hate this company."
- **Undercompensated**: "I know what I'm worth. TechFlow doesn't."
- **Geographically constrained**: "My parents need me nearby. I can't chase Bay Area salaries in NYC."
- **Tired of saying no**: "Every offer requires me to abandon people I trust."

### The Core Tension
> "I'm the most valuable person on this team, and I've made myself impossible to poach. That's either loyalty or stupidity. I can't tell anymore."

## 3. Discovery (Call to Adventure)

### The Trigger
Sarah sees the Crowell & Moring article first—a law firm hiring 40+ attorneys as a unit. She forwards it to Alex with a message: "Is this a thing in tech too?"

Alex's response changes everything: "I've been researching this. Let me show you something."

### Sarah's Initial Reaction
- "Wait, companies would hire all four of us together?"
- "This sounds like acquihire but without selling a company."
- "If this is real, I might actually be able to move."

### What Made It Click
The Groysberg research hit hard:

> "Star performers decline 46% when they move alone."

Sarah thinks: "That's why the Google people I know seem... diminished. They were stars in context. The context didn't transfer."

Then she reads about psychological safety accounting for 43% of team performance. She thinks about all the times she's said "this might be a dumb idea" in team meetings—and it wasn't dumb, and no one made her feel dumb for asking.

That's worth protecting.

## 4. Why Sarah Joined the Liftout Profile (Crossing the Threshold)

### What Alex Pitched Her
Alex came to Sarah first. "I want to explore this, but only if you're in. You're the one everyone's trying to poach. If you're not committed, this doesn't work."

### What Sarah Needed to Hear
1. **"You set the salary floor"**: Alex said Sarah's market rate would anchor the team's ask
2. **"No one moves unless everyone moves"**: All-or-nothing commitment
3. **"Your constraints matter"**: Sarah's geographic limitation (aging parents) would be a team requirement
4. **"You're not just a participant—you're a co-founder"**: Equal voice in decisions

### Sarah's Conditions
Before agreeing, Sarah laid out her terms:
- Minimum salary of $300K for herself (market correction)
- Remote work required (can't relocate from Bay Area)
- Research time—at least 20% for papers/exploration
- Title upgrade: Principal Data Scientist or equivalent

Alex agreed to negotiate for all of these as team requirements.

### The Commitment Moment
Sarah texted Marcus and Priya: "Alex showed me something. I think we can all move together. Are you in?"

Both responded within an hour. The team was formed before they created a single profile.

## 6. Transformation (The Ordeal)

### Sarah's Key Decisions

**Decision 1: Set the compensation anchor**
Sarah insisted her market rate be the baseline for negotiation. "If they're paying team premium, they're paying market rate. Not TechFlow rate."

**Decision 2: Require remote work as team policy**
Sarah's geographic constraint became a team feature: "We're a hybrid-remote team. That's our strength, not a limitation."

**Decision 3: Interview as a unit**
When NextGen wanted to interview her separately, Sarah pushed back through Alex: "I'm not performing solo. Watch us work together."

**Decision 4: Negotiate research time**
Sarah made 20% research time a deal requirement—and got it written into her offer letter.

### Moments of Doubt
- **Week 2**: "What if my parents' health gets worse and I can't even do hybrid?"
- **Week 5**: "The Anthropic offer keeps haunting me. Did I make the right call?"
- **Week 7**: "NextGen seems more interested in Alex than me. Am I just part of the package?"

### The Breakthrough
The joint interview with NextGen. Sarah presented a technical deep-dive on their NLP pipeline. The CTO asked follow-up questions for 40 minutes.

After the call, Alex messaged the team: "They want Sarah as much as they want me. Maybe more."

Sarah realized: She wasn't "part of the package." She was the package.
